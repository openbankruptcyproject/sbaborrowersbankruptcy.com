SBA Borrowers in Bankruptcy

Personal Guarantees, Co-Signers, and Multi-Debtor Strategies

Yes - SBA Debt Is Dischargeable

Every type of SBA loan - 7(a), 504, EIDL, and microloans - can be discharged in bankruptcy. There is no special carve-out for government-guaranteed business debt the way there is for student loans. The SBA guarantee protects the lender from loss, not the borrower from discharge.

But "dischargeable" does not mean simple. SBA loans create a web of liability that can include the business entity, the individual owner, the owner's spouse, co-owners, and sometimes outside co-signers. Each person's liability must be addressed separately. For a complete overview of SBA loans in bankruptcy, see our main SBA borrower bankruptcy guide.

The Personal Guarantee Web

SBA lending rules (SOP 50 10) require personal guarantees from every owner with 20% or more equity in the business. In practice, many lenders also require guarantees from owners below 20%, and sometimes from non-owner family members or business associates.

Each guarantor is jointly and severally liable for the full loan balance. This means the SBA can collect the entire amount from any single guarantor - not just their proportional share. When one guarantor files bankruptcy, the remaining guarantors become even more exposed because the SBA concentrates collection on them.

Critical rule: A bankruptcy discharge only eliminates your obligation. It does not protect co-guarantors. If you file and your business partner does not, your partner is still liable for the entire balance. This is why coordinated multi-party filings are often necessary.

Spouse Liability and Community Property

Whether your spouse is exposed to your SBA debt depends on two factors: whether they signed the guarantee, and which state you live in.

If your spouse signed: They are a co-guarantor and personally liable. Their discharge requires their own bankruptcy filing (or a joint filing with you).

Community property states (Arizona, California, Idaho, Louisiana, Nevada, New Mexico, Texas, Washington, Wisconsin): Even if your spouse did not sign the guarantee, community property assets may be reachable by the SBA. Filing a joint Chapter 7 protects community property through the automatic stay protection in bankruptcy and extends the discharge to community claims.

Common law states: If your spouse did not sign, their separate assets are generally not reachable. However, jointly-owned property (joint bank accounts, jointly-titled real estate) can still be at risk. A joint filing may still be strategic to maximize exemptions.

Business Entity vs Personal Filing

The most common mistake SBA borrowers make is filing only one type of bankruptcy when both are needed. Here is how the pieces fit together:

Business-only filing (Chapter 7 or 11): Addresses the entity's debt. A Chapter 7 liquidates the business. A Chapter 11 or Subchapter V restructures it. Neither eliminates your personal guarantee. The SBA will pursue you individually after the business case closes.

Personal-only filing (Chapter 7 or 13): Eliminates your personal guarantee obligation. The business entity's debt remains. If the business is still operating, this can work - the business continues paying while your personal exposure is eliminated.

Coordinated filing: The most comprehensive approach. The business files Chapter 7 (if closing) or Subchapter V (if continuing), and you file personal Chapter 7 simultaneously. This eliminates all SBA liability in one coordinated action. Courts in the same district can administer both cases efficiently.

What About Collateral?

Discharge eliminates personal liability, but it does not remove liens. If the SBA loan is secured by business equipment, inventory, accounts receivable, or real estate, the lender retains its lien on that collateral even after discharge.

In a Chapter 7, the trustee will liquidate the collateral and pay the secured creditor. In Subchapter V, you can propose to pay the secured claim over time (potentially at a reduced amount through cramdown) while keeping the collateral.

For SBA loans secured by your personal residence: your home is protected by your state's homestead exemption. If your equity is within the exemption limit, you keep the house and the SBA's junior lien can be stripped in Chapter 13 if the home is underwater.

Coordinating Multiple Filings

When an SBA loan involves multiple guarantors (common in partnerships and multi-member LLCs), the timing and coordination of bankruptcy filings is critical:

Simultaneous filing: All guarantors file at the same time. This prevents the SBA from shifting collection to non-filing guarantors. Most efficient when all parties are equally unable to pay.

Sequential filing: One guarantor files first, followed by others. This may be necessary when co-guarantors are in different financial situations or different states. The first filer's discharge eliminates only their obligation - others must file to eliminate theirs.

Chapter 13 co-debtor stay: If you file Chapter 13 (not Chapter 7), the co-debtor stay under 11 U.S.C. 1301 temporarily protects consumer co-debtors from collection. Note: this only applies to consumer debts, and SBA business loans may not qualify. Courts are split on whether personal guarantees of business debt are "consumer" for co-debtor stay purposes.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can SBA debt be discharged in bankruptcy?

Yes. All types of SBA debt - 7(a), 504, EIDL, and microloans - are fully dischargeable in bankruptcy. There is no special exception for government-guaranteed business loans. Both the loan itself and any personal guarantee obligation can be eliminated through discharge.

Does my spouse have to file bankruptcy if I have an SBA loan?

Only if your spouse signed the personal guarantee or is a co-borrower. In community property states (Arizona, California, Idaho, Louisiana, Nevada, New Mexico, Texas, Washington, Wisconsin), community assets may be at risk even if only one spouse signed. Filing jointly can protect both spouses and community property.

What happens to my business partner's liability if I file bankruptcy?

Your bankruptcy discharge only eliminates your personal obligation. Co-guarantors and business partners who also signed personal guarantees remain fully liable for the entire loan balance. The SBA can - and typically does - pursue remaining guarantors after one files bankruptcy.

Can I discharge an SBA loan without closing my business?

Yes. If you file personal Chapter 7, your personal guarantee is discharged but the business entity's obligation remains. Alternatively, filing Subchapter V Chapter 11 allows the business to restructure the SBA loan while continuing operations. The choice depends on whether you want to keep the business running.
